Questions

  1. What is the percentage of a female receiving a gold medal when competing against others of the same gender and how does that compare to men?
    • Women have a 33% chance of receiving a gold medal when competing against other women, while men have a 34% chance of winning a gold when competing against other men. This isn’t a huge difference, in fact women have been competing in the Olympics since 1920 while men have been fighting for the Gold. Basically, women have are at 33% while only been competing for 96 years while men are at only 34% and they have been competing for a whopping 120.
  2. How much more likely is a man to win a gold medal during an Olympic event than a woman under same situation?
    • Out of every since Olympic event men do seem to have a stunning advantage over women, during the same events people from the same country, just different genders have a huge difference in statistical advantage. According to the below data, men have a 36% chance of winning a gold medal during an event over women competing in the same event. I thought that at least different countries will invest more in their athletes, but it seems that too is not fair. It looks like that men have a high chance of bring home the Gold.
  3. Does the season of event change the percentage advantage between men and women during the summer and winter of an Olympic event and does age cause an advantage?
    • According to the data, I found that out of all Olympic athletes, women are surprisingly younger, which I thought would give them the advantage, you know the younger you are the more energy and power you have and the more training you can go though. But apparently that doesn’t matter. With the average age of female athletes being 32 (5 years younger then the average male), men not only still have the advantage of having more gold medals in that age but also have the more older athletes still competing. Men max out at 66 while the oldest female athlete caps out at 52 years of age.
